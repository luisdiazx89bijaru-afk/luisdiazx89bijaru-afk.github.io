The Inscription That Recorded the Fall of Mithras: Archaeologists Find Jesus' Name at a Roman Mystery Temple

Published on Archaeology & History News | Estimated Era: Late 3rd – Early 4th Century AD

📍 Location: Zerzevan Castle (Ancient Roman Frontier Fortress), Diyarbakır Province, Southeastern Turkey.

🔍 Discovery Type: Deciphered Aramaic Inscription & Ritual Closure Evidence.

One of the most remarkable archaeological discoveries of recent years has just rewritten the history of religious transition within the Roman Empire. Deep inside the famous underground Mithraeum at Zerzevan Castle, researchers have successfully decoded a previously mysterious Aramaic inscription carved directly near the entrance of the subterranean sanctuary.

The text has left the global scientific community astonished: it explicitly mentions both the pagan deity Mithras and Jesus Christ, accompanied by a deeply carved Christian cross.

The Discovery: From Silent Abandonment to Ritual Confrontation

For decades, historians and archaeologists suspected that this remote military outpost's sanctuary had simply fallen into decay and eventual abandonment as the Roman Empire shifted its geopolitical focus. However, this newly decoded inscription reveals a far more dramatic reality.

The physical evidence strongly suggests that the sanctuary was subject to a deliberate, ritualistic closure during the rapid expansion of early Christianity along the eastern Roman frontier. This find is now believed to be the first known archaeological artifact in the world that explicitly documents the symbolic sealing of a Mithraic temple while directly invoking the name of Christ.

Historical Impact: A Window into the Spiritual Revolution

Very few archaeological discoveries capture the volatile religious revolution of Late Antiquity as vividly as this inscription. Rather than presenting an abstract theological shift or showing Christianity simply replacing paganism centuries later through slow assimilation, the stone walls of Zerzevan capture the raw moment of transition itself.

This fortress was a place where Roman soldiers and civilians—who for generations swore oaths to Mithras—witnessed firsthand the exact era when Christianity grew to become the dominant faith of the Empire. Instead of reading about history in texts, archaeologists have uncovered the physical remnants of a profound religious confrontation, forever preserved in stone on the ancient edge of Rome.
